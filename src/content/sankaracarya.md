# Adi Shankaracharya

Name: *Ādi Śaṅkarācārya*  
Pronunciation: *Aa-di Shun-ka-raa-chaa-rya*  
Meaning: *The first teacher named Śaṅkara*  
Title: *Enlightened soul and reviver of Advaita Vedānta*  

Sri Adi Shankaracharya stands among these luminaries as an *Avatara* of *Lord Shiva* Himself, whose appearance twelve centuries ago transformed the spiritual landscape of India, revitalised the profound wisdom of *Vedanta*, and forged national integration across diverse regions. If *Sanatana Dharma* thrives today, it is primarily due to Sri Shankaracharya's intervention and tireless work. 

In an era where the pristine wisdom of *Sanatana Dharma* faced unprecedented peril, and the Vedic path to liberation lay obscured beneath divisive sectarianism and materialistic philosophies. The Hindu society was fractured into countless competing sects, each with its contradictory interpretations and endless disputations, weakening the unified spiritual foundation.

Sri Shankaracharya emerged as the divine response to humanity's spiritual crisis. He consolidated the doctrine of *Advaita Vedanta* and re-established the authority of the *Vedas*. In his first 16 years, he studied the *Vedas, Upanishads, and Shastras*, and wrote commentaries, *stotras*, and other texts that demonstrated his profound intellect. 

Later, by travelling across various parts of India, he preached his teachings, debated with scholars, established four *mathas* (monastic institutions), founded the *Dashanami monastic order*, unified the *Shanmata* tradition of worship, and introduced the *Panchayatana puja*, thus bringing together separate ideologies into a unified tradition. 

Shankaracharya wrote commentaries on the *Prasthanatrayi* - the *Brahmasutras (Nyaya Prasthana)*, the ten Upanishads (*Shruti Prasthana*) and the *Bhagavad Gita (Smruti Prasthana)*. Hence, the title of *Acharya* was given, and he was known as *Shankaracharya*. Since he was the first propagator of Advaita Vedanta, he was known as Adi. 

He lived till the age of 32. His philosophies and ideals continue to shape our country till today.

---

## Essence of his teaching

- *Brahman* is the only reality. The individual soul (*jiva*) is fundamentally non-different from Brahman.

- Liberation (*Moksha*) is the direct recognition of this eternal oneness.

- Action (*Karma*) and Devotion (*Bhakti*), when performed with proper understanding, purify the mind and prepare it for this supreme knowledge.

- There are many forms of God, but essentially they are *One*.

## Birth

Shankaracharya was born in the village of Sasalam, located near the Poorna (also known as Periyar) river, to the devout couple *Shivaguru* and *Aryamba*. Shivaguru was not interested in marriage, but due to his guru’s advice, he married. Aryamba belonged to a *Namboodri* brahmin family living at Melpazhur Mana in the city of Veliyanad, near Piravom in the Ernakulam district of Kerala. 

However, the couple didn’t have a child till old age. The couple went to the *Vadakkumnathan Temple, Thrissur* (known as Tri-Shiva-puram) and prayed to Shiva for 40 days for a son. Shiva appeared in their dream and gave them two options: one ordinary son with a long life or an extraordinary son but short-lived. They chose the latter, and Shankara was born in his maternal home. 

Young Shankara displayed extraordinary intelligence and spiritual inclination. By the age of eight, Shankara had known the entire Vedic corpus. His father died when he was only around three.

## Gurukul life and wonders

- *Poorna River changed its path*: Shankara went to the gurukul near Sasalam. Once, his mother fainted while she was fetching water from the Poorna river as it was at a distance. Shankara, with the help of his feet and ankle, created a path in the sand, and the river changed its track and flew near his home. Afterwards, the place was known as *Kalady* (kaal + atti = foot + eddi).

- *Raining of golden almas*: While in gurukul, he once went to a house to beg for his daily food. A very poor old woman was living there. Unable to give anything, she offered an amla fruit lying on the ground under the tree. Deeply touched by her poverty, Shankara recited the *kanaka-dhara stotra*. Suddenly, golden alma fell from the tree, showing the divine blessing of goddess Lakshmi.

- *Becoming a sannyasi*: Shankara was deeply interested in becoming a *sannyasi*. But her mother was reluctant to permit her only son. Once while bathing, a crocodile caught hold of Shankara. Seeing his imminent death, he pleaded with his mother to grant him permission and a blessing to be a sannyasi. As soon as his mother granted permission, the crocodile left his grasp and went away. Sankara became a sannyasi, but promised his mother he could come back to her at the time of her death and perform the last rites. Upon the advice of a brahmana, he went to the banks of the *Narmada* River to meet his guru, Govindapada.

- *Govindapada’s blessings*: when Shankara reached his guru, he recited another stotra, *Nirvana Shatakam*, when his guru asked about his identity. Pleased, *Shri Govindapada* accepted him as his disciple. Once during the rainy season, his guru was meditating inside the cave and was in samadhi. The level of Narmada rose, and other disciples began to fear that the waters would drown Govindpada. No one wanted to disturb their guru’s meditation. Shankara went and placed his kamandalu at the entrance of the cave. Then he recited the Narmada ashtakam to appease Narmada and prayed to let her water be absorbed in that *kamandalu*. Surprisingly, the water level never rose further. Seeing this, his guru told him to write commentaries containing the essence of the *Vedic scriptures*. That would obtain him eternal glory.

- *Debate with Sage Vyasa and blessings*: While Shankara was travelling with his disciples, a wandering sage of dark skin challenged him to a debate (*Shastrartha*) on brahma sutras. The debate went on for 18 days. Extremely pleased, the sage introduced himself as Sage Vyasa and increased Shankara’s life span by another *16 years*. Then Vyasa asked him to travel the entire country and preach the Advaita Vedanta. He also asked him to defeat the people in Shastrartha who only believed in rituals and not the underlying principle of Advaita Vedanta. Vyasa asked him to go first to the famous *Kumarila Bhatta* and challenge him to a debate.

---

## Debate with Mandana Misra

- As *Kumarila Bhatta* was about to give up his life, he asked Shankara to go to *Kashi* and challenge *Mandana Misra*. So he went to Kashi and challenged Mandana. Mandana’s wife, Ubhaya Bharati, became the judge. Both wore flower garlands, and it was decided that whoever whose garland withered first would be considered defeated, as it was the sign of loss of intellectual vitality and composure during the debate.

- The debate went on for 16 days, and finally, Mandana Misra accepted defeat. Seeing his husband’s defeat, *Ubhaya Bharati* challenged Shankara and asked him questions regarding *Kama Shastra*. Since Shankara was unfamiliar with the subject, he asked for one month to study the Kama Sutra. He went into the body of a dead king (*Amaruka*) and learned everything. After that, Shankara debated with Ubhaya Bharati and defeated her as well. Both Mandana Misra and Ubhaya Bharati became disciples of Shankara.

## Establishing 4 mathas

- Shankara had four main disciples: *Mandana, Padmapada, Hastamalaka and Giri (or Trotaka)*. Each of them was made head of one matha.  

- Shankara chose to establish four mathas in four directions. He chose *Sringeri* when he saw a cobra shading a pregnant frog, symbolising harmony beyond natural hostility. He chose *Puri* as it was an ancient centre of Jagannath worship. Then chose *Badrinath* as it was the seat of Sage Vyasa and Himalayan wisdom. He chose *Dwaraka* as it was associated with Krishna. The four mathas allowed *Jnana* (knowledge), *bhakti* (devotion), *karma* (ritual) and *sannyasa* (renunciation).

## Founder

- *Dashanami monastic order*: Adi Shankara organised the Hindu monastics of these ten sects or names under four mathas. A sannyasi of Swami order has dual significance, representing the attainment of supreme bliss through some divine quality or state (i.e. love, wisdom, service, yoga), and through a harmony with the infinite vastness of nature, expressed in one of the *ten subdivision* names: *Giri* (mountain), *Puri* (tract), *Bharati* (land), *Vana* (forest), *Aranya* (forest), *Sagara* (sea), *Ashrama* (spiritual exertion), *Sarasvati* (wisdom of nature), *Tirtha* (place of pilgrimage), and *Parvata* (mountain range).

- *Unifying the Shanmata tradition of worship*: This tradition centres around the worship of six primary deities of Hinduism: *Shiva, Vishnu, Shakti, Ganesha, Surya, and Skanda*. It is based on the belief in the essential oneness of all deities, the unity of Godhead, and their conceptualisation of the myriad deities of India as various manifestations of the one divine power, *Para Brahman*.

- *Introduced Panchayatana puja*: Previously, different sects had their own deities. Shankaracharya introduced the *Panchayatana* puja, where everyone worships 5 deities, i.e., Vishnu, Shiva, Shakti, Ganesha and Surya. Depending on the tradition followed by the households, one of these deities is kept in the centre and the other four corners of a square surrounding it.

- Shankaracharya also established the *Naga sadhus* to protect Sanatana Dharma, and that sect was trained in martial arts and weapons.

---

## Literary Works

- *Prasthana-traya bhasya*: Shankara wrote the *Brahmasutras bhasya* (Nyaya Prasthana), bhasyas on the ten Upanishads (Shruti Prasthana) and the *Bhagavad Gita bhasya* (Smruti Prasthana). The 10 Upanishads are Isa, Kena, Katha, Prasna, Mundaka, Mandukya, Taittiriya, Aitareya, Chandogya, and Brhadaranyaka.

- *Prakarana Grantha*: Widely accepted are Upadesha Sahasri, Viveka-chudamani, Atma Bodha, Tattva Bodha, Aparokshanubhuti, Panchikarana, Laghu Vakyavritti, Vakyavritti, Satshloki, Dasashloki, Prabodha Sudhakara, Sarva Vedanta Siddhanta Sara Sangraha, etc.

- *Stotras & hymns*: Sankaracharya has written many stotras. Some of the popular ones are *Bhaja Govindam, Nirvana Shatakam, Kanaka-dhara stotra, Mahisasura Mardini Stotram, Lakshmi Narasimha Karavalamba Stotram, Ganga Stotram, and Narmada Ashtakam*. He also composed hymns on Shiva, Vishnu, Ganesha, Subrahmanya, Sharada, Saraswati, Lakshmi, Narasimha, including *Shivananda Lahiri, Soundarya Lahiri*, and all the time remained a teacher until he reached the thirty-second year of his life.

---

## Return to his mother

- Shankaracharya went to his mother, who was on her deathbed. Shankara showed her mother the form of *Mahavishnu*, and she attained liberation. In some accounts, it's mentioned that the local brahmins didn’t allow Shankara to cremate her mother’s body as he was a sannyasi and didn’t give him the woods. However, Shankara used the stems of banana plants to cremate her body.

- Shankaracharya went to Kashmir to the Sarvajna Peetham to seek the darshan of goddess *Sharada*.

## Words of Wisdom

## Footnotes

- *Advaita Vedanta*: A non-dual philosophical system teaching that the individual self (Ātman) and the absolute reality (Brahman) are essentially one. 

- *Avatara*: A divine descent or incarnation of God on earth, undertaken to restore dharma suited to address the spiritual crises of their time. Vishnu’s incarnations, such as Rāma and Krishna, are classic examples.

- *Santana Dharma*: Dharma means that which supports. Sanatana means eternal. Overall, it means the set of moral rules or values that is true for every human being. For example, we should hurt no one, we should be humble, speak truth, show humility, respect others, etc. 

- *Vedas*: The oldest sacred scriptures, comprising Rig, Yajur, Sama, and Atharva Veda. Each Veda has 4 main sections: Samhitas, Brahmanas, Aranyakas, and Upanishads. Samhitas consist of mantras and hymns for worship. Brahmanas are commentaries that explain rituals and their significance. Aranyakas discuss the philosophy behind rituals. Lastly, Upanishads are philosophical texts that focus on Atman (individual soul) and Brahman (the Absolute Reality).

- *Shastras*: The texts of the Science (of the Supreme Reality or Brahman), which explain the theory and technique, are called the Shastras.

- *Stotras*: Devotional hymns composed in praise of deities or spiritual truths.

- *Mathas*: Monastic institutions established to preserve spiritual learning and lineage traditions. Shankaracharya’s four principal mathas serve as centres of Advaita teaching.

- *Vadakkumnathan Temple*: An ancient Shiva temple in Thrissur, Kerala.

- *Kanaka-dhara Stotra*: A hymn attributed to invoking Goddess Lakshmi to shower grace and prosperity.

- *Narmada ashtakam*: A devotional hymn praising the sacred river Narmada.
Govindapada:

- *Nirvana Shatakam*: A six-verse hymn attributed to Shankaracharya declaring the identity of the Self with pure consciousness beyond worldly attributes.

- *Vedic Scriptures*: A broad term referring collectively to the Vedas and related texts.

- *Sage Vyasa*: The compiler of the Vedas and author of the Mahabharata and Puranas. He divided the Vedas into four. Vyasa is a title, and the name of Vyasa is Krishna Dwaipayana.

- *Kumarila Bhatta*: A prominent Mimamsa philosopher who defended Vedic authority and ritual tradition against rival schools.

- *Mandana Misra*: A disciple of Kumarila Bhatta, a renowned scholar and householder philosopher who debated Shankaracharya and later became his disciple, known as Sureshvaracharya. He became the head of the South Matha at Sringeri.

- *Ubhaya Bharati*: Wife of Mandana Misra, often accepted as an incarnation of Goddess Saraswati.

- *Kama Shastra*: Texts dealing with aesthetics, relationships, and the principles of love and worldly enjoyment, most famously represented by the Kamasutra.

- *Amaruka*: A king who was dead. To understand the Kama Shastra, Shankaracharya entered his body for one month.

- *Padmapada*: A foremost disciple of Shankaracharya and head of the Govardhana Matha at Puri.

- *Hastamalaka*: One of Shankaracharya's principal disciples, famed for his spontaneous realisation of the Self. He became the head of the West Matha at Dwarka.

- *Trotaka*: A devoted disciple of Shankaracharya who composed the Totakashtakam. He became the head of the North Matha at Badrinath.

- *Shiva, Vishnu, Shakti, Ganesha, Surya, Skanda*: Six deities of Hindu tradition. Skanda is referred to as Karthikeya, son of Shiva and the general of the army of the gods.

- *Para Brahman*: The Absolute Reality, beyond attributes and form.

- *Naga sadhus*: Ascetic warriors belonging to monastic orders, known for renunciation, discipline, and protection of dharma.

- *Prakarana Grantha*: The books that explain the terms used in the Shastra texts are called Prakarana Granthas.
